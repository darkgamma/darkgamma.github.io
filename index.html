Ūlari

a Northern Lowlands language

The Ūlari people have been for a long time described as nomadic or semi-nomadic, though with the advent of irrigation and rice agriculture in the Northern Lowlands, they have switched to a more sedentary lifestyle, aided by the new infrastructure. Culturally, the Ūlari belong to the same dragon herding stock as Mestians and the rest of the main constitutive Imperial peoples, but are otherwise one of only a few remaining constitutive peoples that still practice full-scale dragon husbandry. As such, their lives and livelihood generally involve herding practices, and livestock is traditionally extremely important to them. Traditionally, households amongst the Ūlari have measured wealth in heads of dragon cattle, and this practice persists in some form even today, despite the penetration of monetary economy into herder societies.

The Ūlari language typologically resembles the average herder stock: it is very synthetic, and has a very well developed noun class system. Atypically for herder languages (though shared with its very closest neighbours), the Ūlari noun class system includes a grammaticalised human classification system based of human sex characteristics (or, more generally, a "typical" noun gender system), alongside a more general classification scheme.

Ūlari distinguishes six general noun classes: animate, feminine, plant, inanimate, neuter and ignic. The main feature in Ūlari noun classification is the concept of "specialised" noun classes: nouns and adjectives generally get marked as animate, inanimate, neuter and ignic (being the basic noun classes), but may be "overmarked" for plant and feminine classes, specialising the category of animate and inanimate classes. Gender assignment in the Ūlari is deeply cultural: the trio of animate, inanimate and neuter respectively marks living beings, inanimate matter (as well as former animates), and abstract/intangible concepts; ignic nouns are generally unconnected to animacy and have typical attributes associated with fire or high temperature (with plenty of culturally significant exceptions, such as ghała "breath; soul, spirit"); plant nouns are classified based on their visible characteristics, and, as such, not all botanical plants get marked as classificatory plants. The primary classificatory curiosity is in the marking of feminine nouns.

Ūlari nouns take a feminine specialisation if they are either associated with femininity, or are beings with pronounced features associated with traditional femininity. This marking doesn't reflect actual biological sex, but rather reflects the observable characteristics of creatures. Thus, living beings, and specifically humans, can be unmarked for femininity if they are, for example, young and sexually immature, or have prominent characteristics associated with masculinity (which is otherwise unmarked in Ūlari). Thus, traditionally feminine women will almost certainly be marked as such, and the lack of a feminine marker may be used as a diminutive (reflecting the lack of marking on children), an insult for particularly feminine women, or those who take pride in their femininity, or as a comparison mechanism (when comparing two women), as well as a large number of other, pragmatically determined uses. Biologically male organisms, and specifically men, can also be marked as feminine if they conform to expectations of femininity or behave in a particular manner. This isn't necessarily always insulting, and may be used in a neutral manner as well.

Ūlari has a phonology that is characteristic for Northern Lowlands herder languages. It places a high functional load on sonorants and distinguishes voicing in obstruents and laterals. It distinguishes both consonant and vowel length, but doesn't contrast rounding in vowels (unlike several other prominent herder and regional languages).

To start off, the consonant phoneme inventory:

Plosives: /p b t d t̻ d̻ k g q ʔ/
Fricatives: /ɸ β s z s̻ z̻ ʂ ʐ x/
Affricates: /ts̻ dz̻/
Nasals: /m n ɲ ŋ/
Approximants: /w ð r j ɣ/
Laterals: /l l̥ ʎ ʎ̥ ʟ ʟ̥/

Of these consonants, only /ʔ x ð/ cannot be geminate, and further only /ʔ ð/ cannot occur word-initially. I've chosen to romanise them as:

Plosives: <p b t d ty dy k g q x>
Fricatives: <f v s z sy zy š ž h>
Affricates: <c j>
Nasals: <m n ny nw>
Approximants: <w ð r y gh>
Laterals: <l ł ly ły lw łw>

Geminates are written with a double grapheme; geminates of digraphs duplicate only the first letter. The only exceptions are the geminates /l̥l̥ ʎ̥ʎ̥ ʟ̥ʟ̥/ which are represented as <lh lhy lhw>. The approximants /w y/ are written <u i> after consonants.

Ūlari generally allows only one consonant in the onset, and a maximum of two in the coda. Internally, a sequence of CCC occurs only if the first two CC represent a geminate.

The vowel inventory is somewhat simpler:

High: /i i: u u:/
Mid: /e ɛ e: ɛ: o ɔ o: ɔ:/
Low: /a̟ a̟: ɑ ɑ:/
Diphthongs: /ɑi̯ au̯ ɔɑ̯ ɛa̯ iɛ̯ uɔ̯/

These are represented as:

High: <i ii u uu>
Mid: <ë e ëë ee ö o öö oo>
Low: <ä ää a aa>
Diphthongs: <ai au oa ea ie uo>

Ūlari also has a mora-based pitch accent system, where any one (rarely two) mora of a word may take a phonemic pitch accent feature. This is marked with an acute on the mora in question, such as in <aáða> and similar words; if the grapheme is one of <ë ä ö> (possessing a diaeresis diacritic), a caron is used instead, giving <êunë>. Diphthongs may only be accented on the first segment. The pitch accent feature is most commonly a phonetic pitch upstep, and words that lack the pitch feature start with the final pitch of the previous word. Pitch in Ūlari tends to fall, and drops off rapidly immediately after an upstep. Stress tends to follow the pitch feature, and all accented words will have stress on the accented syllables. Accentless words generally have penultimate, weak stress.

Ūlari has small vowel harmony features: if the first root syllable of a word has any one of <e o>, the word cannot take suffixes with <ë ö> (but may have those vowels in the root itself) which are, instead, changed then to <e o> respectively. This doesn't apply to words whose first root vowels are <ë ö> and their suffixes may take the full range of <e ë o ö> without harmonical modifications.
